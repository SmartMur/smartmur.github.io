    Need Statement: Draft for Grants¶

    Based on focus groups with 52 immigrant mothers in New York City and March 2026 intake tracking

    🎯 For Grant Proposals¶

    This section can be adapted for any grant's "Problem Statement" or "Need" section. Mix and match paragraphs and quotes based on word count.

    📊 The Data Summary¶

    Community: Immigrant mothers in New York City Engagement: 5 focus groups, 52 participants Languages: Arabic, Spanish, Punjabi, Tagalog, Mandarin, Tamil, English Key Finding: 84% cite childcare as primary barrier; 86% want job placement, not just training Current Cycle Check (March 2026): 5 of first 6 confirmed participants requested childcare support

    📝 Draft 1: Comprehensive (500 words)¶

    The Hidden Barriers Immigrant Mothers Face¶

    Immigrant mothers arrive in Canada with resilience, determination, and dreams for their children. Yet they face overlapping barriers that keep them from economic opportunity—barriers that traditional training programs fail to address.

    The Childcare Trap

    For mothers of young children, attending any program requires solving an impossible equation. With family often thousands of miles away and childcare costs consuming what little income they might earn, most simply cannot participate.

    "I have no one. My family is all back home. If I go somewhere, my baby comes with me or I don't go."

    This reality excludes immigrant mothers from workforce development opportunities before they even begin. Of 52 women consulted, 84% identified childcare as the single greatest barrier to learning new skills.

    The Confidence Gap

    Strikingly, the women we spoke with already possess raw ability—most use smartphones, navigate apps, and support their children's learning. What holds them back is not capability but belief.

    "I'm 45. Isn't that too old to learn computers?"

    Seventy-two percent described feeling "not smart enough" or "too old" to learn technology. This confidence gap, not skills gap, is the true barrier.

    The Job Connection

    Women are skeptical of programs that teach skills without connecting to employment. They've seen friends complete training only to remain jobless.

    "I don't want to learn just to learn. I want a job at the end."

    Eighty-six percent said job placement support was essential—not optional.

    The Isolation Factor

    Many immigrant mothers experience profound isolation. Without family networks and cut off from professional communities, they lack both support and connections to opportunity.

    "I'm alone here. No family. I want to be with women who understand."

    The Access Gap

    While most have smartphones, few have laptops or reliable internet for serious learning. Sixty-two percent lack adequate devices; 54% have unreliable internet.

    "My husband takes the computer to work. I have nothing during the day."

    The System Failure

    Existing programs fail because they're designed for a different reality. Evening classes assume women have no childcare responsibilities. English-only instruction excludes those building language skills. Individual learning models ignore the power of community.

    The result is a population of talented, motivated women locked out of the digital economy—not by ability, but by systems that weren't built for them.

    The Opportunity

    When immigrant mothers gain technology skills and economic independence, families transform. Children see their mothers working. Income increases. Communities strengthen.

    But only if we design for their actual lives.

    📝 Draft 2: Concise (250 words)¶

    What Immigrant Mothers Told Us¶

    When we asked 52 immigrant mothers what stands between them and economic opportunity, their answers were clear—and surprising.

    Childcare is the unseen barrier. Eighty-four percent said they cannot attend any program without childcare support. With family abroad and costs prohibitive, most simply don't participate in existing offerings.

    "I have no one. If I go somewhere, my baby comes with me or I don't go."

    Confidence, not skills, is the real gap. Women already use smartphones and apps. What holds them back is belief—72% described feeling "too old" or "not smart enough" to learn technology.

    Jobs, not just training, is the goal. Eighty-six percent said job placement support is essential. They've seen too many programs promise skills and deliver no employment.

    "I don't want to learn just to learn. I want a job at the end."

    Existing programs don't fit their lives. Evening classes assume no childcare. English-only instruction excludes language learners. Individual models ignore the power of community.

    The result: talented, motivated women locked out of the digital economy—not by ability, but by programs designed for someone else's reality.

    📝 Draft 3: Quote-Heavy (for compelling narrative)¶

    In Their Own Words¶

    The Revelation Project began by listening. Over five focus groups, 52 immigrant mothers told us what stands between them and economic opportunity.

    On childcare:

    "I have no one. My family is all back home. If I go somewhere, my baby comes with me or I don't go."

    On confidence:

    "I'm 45. Isn't that too old to learn computers?"

    "I tried once. I couldn't figure it out. I felt so dumb I never tried again."

    On what they actually want:

    "I don't want to learn just to learn. I want a job at the end."

    "If you can connect me to real employers, I'll do anything."

    On community:

    "I'm alone here. No family. I want to be with women who understand."

    "We need each other. That's how we survive."

    On their dreams:

    "I want my daughter to see me working. To be proud."

    "I want to open my own business. Help other women like me."

    These voices reveal a truth that data alone cannot capture: immigrant mothers possess the motivation, the resilience, and the dreams. What they lack are programs designed for their actual lives.
