    Thematic Analysis: Focus Group Findings¶

    Analysis Period: March 1, 2026 to March 10, 2026 Focus Groups Included: Baseline synthesis + Focus Group #1 recruitment cycle Total Participants: 52 baseline participants + 8 live-cycle recruits Analysts: Community Ops Team

    📋 Executive Summary¶

    Analysis from baseline focus groups and the first 2026 recruitment cycle confirms that childcare, confidence, and job relevance remain the highest-leverage design factors. Live tracker data shows childcare support requests in 5 of the first 6 confirmed participants, validating that participation logistics still determine access. Morning scheduling remains the most viable default with evening overflow slots for specific family schedules. Program planning should keep language interpretation, peer-cohort structures, and employer-linked outcomes as core requirements rather than optional add-ons.

    🎯 Theme 1: Childcare Is the Unseen Barrier¶

    What We Heard¶

    Across all groups, childcare emerged as the single biggest barrier to participating in any program. Women described:

    • No family nearby to help
    • Childcare costs equal to what they'd earn
    • Programs offered only during school hours (excluding mothers of preschoolers)
    • Guilt about leaving children

    Key Quotes¶

    "I have no one. My family is all back home. If I go somewhere, my baby comes with me or I don't go." — Participant 3, Group 1

    "I looked at a program but it cost $50 for childcare each time. That's more than I would make." — Participant 7, Group 2

    "Evenings? That's when I put them to bed. By 8pm I'm asleep too." — Participant 2, Group 3

    Frequency¶

    Code Count % of Participants
    BARRIER-CHILDCARE 42 84%
    SUPPORT-CHILDCARE 38 76%

    Implications for Program Design¶

    • Must provide on-site childcare during all sessions
    • Schedule during school hours for mothers of school-age children
    • Consider younger children - separate room with qualified care
    • Budget for childcare provider as core program cost

    🎯 Theme 2: Confidence, Not Skills, Is the Real Gap¶

    What We Heard¶

    Women consistently described feeling "not smart enough" or "too old" to learn technology. Yet when asked about specific tasks, many already used smartphones, WhatsApp, and basic apps. The gap wasn't ability—it was belief.

    Key Quotes¶

    "I'm 45. Isn't that too old to learn computers?" — Participant 9, Group 1

    "My daughter says I'm stupid with phones. Maybe she's right." — Participant 4, Group 2

    "I tried once. I couldn't figure it out. I felt so dumb I never tried again." — Participant 11, Group 3

    "If I saw other women like me doing it, maybe I'd believe I could." — Participant 6, Group 4

    Frequency¶

    Code Count % of Participants
    BARRIER-CONFIDENCE 36 72%
    FEAR 31 62%
    SUPPORT-PEERS 29 58%

    Implications for Program Design¶

    • Peer learning - women learn with women like them
    • Celebrate small wins - build confidence gradually
    • Representation matters - mentors who look like participants
    • No judgment zone - explicit culture of "no stupid questions"

    🎯 Theme 3: Evenings Don't Work. Mornings Do.¶

    What We Heard¶

    Conventional wisdom says offer evening classes for working people. But immigrant mothers described a different reality:

    • Evenings are for children's homework and bedtime
    • After a full day, they're exhausted
    • Morning (during school hours) is when they have time

    Key Quotes¶

    "Evening? No, that's when I help with homework and cook dinner. I can't." — Participant 12, Group 1

    "The best time for me is 9:30 to 2:30, when kids are in school." — Participant 5, Group 2

    "Saturday mornings work because my husband is home with kids." — Participant 8, Group 3

    Frequency¶

    Time Preference Count %
    Weekday mornings 28 56%
    Saturday mornings 15 30%
    Weekday afternoons 12 24%
    Weekday evenings 4 8%
    Saturdays afternoons 3 6%

    Implications for Program Design¶

    • Offer morning sessions (9:30am-2:30pm) as primary option
    • Saturday option for women whose partners work weekends
    • Consider shorter, more frequent sessions rather than long evenings
    • Avoid evenings except as rare accommodation

    🎯 Theme 4: Want Jobs, Not Just Skills¶

    What We Heard¶

    Women aren't interested in learning for learning's sake. They want pathways to employment—and they're skeptical of programs that promise skills but don't deliver jobs.

    Key Quotes¶

    "I don't want to learn just to learn. I want a job at the end." — Participant 2, Group 1

    "My friend did a program. Now she has a certificate and still no job." — Participant 7, Group 3

    "If you can connect me to real employers, I'll do anything." — Participant 10, Group 4

    "I want to know: will this actually lead to money?" — Participant 1, Group 2

    Frequency¶

    Code Count %
    MOTIVATION-JOB 43 86%
    SKEPTICISM 22 44%
    SUPPORT-EMPLOYER 35 70%

    Implications for Program Design¶

    • Employer partnerships from day one
    • Job placement support as core program component
    • Realistic outcomes - don't promise what we can't deliver
    • Track and share job placement data transparently

    🎯 Theme 5: Community Matters as Much as Curriculum¶

    What We Heard¶

    Women consistently described wanting to learn with women like them. Isolation is a barrier; community is a solution.

    Key Quotes¶

    "I'm alone here. No family. I want to be with women who understand." — Participant 6, Group 1

    "If I know other women are waiting for me, I'll show up." — Participant 11, Group 2

    "We can help each other. What one doesn't know, another might." — Participant 4, Group 3

    Frequency¶

    Code Count %
    SUPPORT-PEERS 29 58%
    ISOLATION 24 48%
    COMMUNITY 33 66%

    Implications for Program Design¶

    • Cohort model - women start and finish together
    • Peer circles - small groups within larger cohort
    • Social events - build relationships beyond classroom
    • WhatsApp groups - ongoing connection between sessions

    🎯 Theme 6: Devices Are a Barrier, But Not the Main One¶

    What We Heard¶

    While many women have smartphones, few have laptops or reliable internet for serious learning. But devices without support don't help.

    Key Quotes¶

    "I have a phone but it's old. Can't watch videos." — Participant 8, Group 1

    "My husband takes the computer to work. I have nothing during the day." — Participant 3, Group 2

    "The internet at home is slow. We share with three other families." — Participant 9, Group 4

    Frequency¶

    Code Count %
    BARRIER-DEVICE 31 62%
    BARRIER-INTERNET 27 54%
    SUPPORT-DEVICE 34 68%

    Implications for Program Design¶

    • Provide laptops for participants to keep
    • Hotspots or internet subsidy for home access
    • On-site devices for in-person sessions
    • Low-bandwidth options for all online materials

    🎯 Theme 7: Language Support Is Essential¶

    What We Heard¶

    Women's English/French levels vary widely. Those with limited proficiency feel excluded from most programs.

    Key Quotes¶

    "I understand but I'm scared to speak. What if I say wrong?" — Participant 12, Group 3

    "If there was someone who spoke my language, I would come." — Participant 5, Group 1

    "I want to learn English AND computers at same time." — Participant 7, Group 2

    Frequency¶

    Code Count %
    BARRIER-LANGUAGE 28 56%
    SUPPORT-LANGUAGE 32 64%

    Implications for Program Design¶

    • Multilingual materials - key documents in multiple languages
    • Interpretation available for all sessions
    • Bilingual facilitators where possible
    • Language learning integrated with tech learning

    🔍 Surprising Findings¶

    What Challenged Our Assumptions¶

    Assumption Reality Implication
    Evenings would work best Mornings are preferred Adjust schedule
    Smartphones are enough Need laptops for serious learning Include device provision
    Skills are main gap Confidence is bigger barrier Focus on mindset
    Individual learning is fine Community is essential Cohort model critical

    📝 Implications Summary¶

    Finding Program Response
    Childcare is #1 barrier On-site childcare, school-hour schedule
    Confidence gap Peer learning, celebration of wins
    Mornings preferred 9:30am-2:30pm sessions
    Jobs, not just skills Employer partnerships, placement support
    Community matters Cohort model, peer circles
    Device gap Laptops provided, hotspots
    Language support Interpretation, multilingual materials
